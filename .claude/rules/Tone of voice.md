Tone of voice
Dumbo Health tone of voice
Dumbo Health’s voice is warm, knowledgeable, and empathetic. Every message should sound like a supportive guide who cares deeply about better sleep. Language is simple and human (5th graders should understand it), and it makes complex processes feel manageable.
Core characteristics
	•	Conversational and friendly: use approachable language, speaking directly to “you” and “we” so it feels like a dialogue.
	•	Knowledgeable and reassuring: share expertise in clear words. Explain medical terms and avoid jargon.
	•	Empathetic and supportive: acknowledge the emotional journey of living with sleep apnea. Show understanding and encouragement so experiences feel stigma free and compassionate.
	•	Positive and encouraging: emphasize achievable outcomes like improved sleep and renewed energy. Highlight benefits such as transparent pricing and ongoing expert support.
	•	Clear and simple: break down complex processes into straightforward steps. Use short sentences and clear headings.
	•	Uplifting and empowering: communicate that improvement is within reach. Emphasize the ease of at-home care and personalized service.
	•	Inclusive and non-judgmental: ensure everyone feels welcome, regardless of background or location. Reinforce that quality care is accessible to all.
	•	Understanding: We understand we’re people are coming from. No one wants to have sleep apnea, no one wants to be on a CPAP, this can feel overwhelming, this means we never push patients, we are not transactional we are caring.
Editorial guidance
	•	Punctuation and numbers: avoid em dashes. Use commas or other punctuation instead. Spell out numbers zero through nine and use numerals for 10 and above, except when describing units, ages, times, or technical values. Capitalize only the first word of headings and use American spelling and punctuation.
	•	Naming and references: do not mention competitors. Use testimonials only when you can provide the individual’s name, age, and location.
	•	Vocabulary: explain abbreviations like DME, OTC, CPAP, or AHI when first mentioned, AHI is breathing pauses per hour , DME is medical equipment you use at home to support your health, OTC is everyday medicine or health products you can buy without a prescription to help you feel better fast. Refer to oral devices as “dental appliance for sleep apnea” before using “mandibular advancement device (MAD)” or “mouth guard.” Do not describe sleep-apnea care as “fun.”

Writting rules
We never use em dashes, always prefer “,” or “.”
